**Send TODAY. Call first, then deliver this signed letter (email + hand-delivery). Keep a copy.**

**Re: [PATIENT FULL NAME], DOB [PATIENT DOB], date of death [PATIENT DOD] — INSTRUCTION TO HOLD EMBALMING**

To whom it may concern,

I am [PERSONAL REPRESENTATIVE NAME], the [surviving spouse / next of kin / personal representative] of [PATIENT FULL NAME], who died on [PATIENT DOD]. I am providing this written instruction in accordance with my authority over the disposition of his remains.

**The remains are NOT to be embalmed at this time.**

The remains are to be **maintained under refrigeration only** until I provide further written authorization. The family is in the process of determining whether to request an autopsy — either through the [COUNTY OF DEATH] County Medical Examiner or through a private forensic-pathology service. **Embalming would permanently compromise toxicology, microbiology, and tissue analysis** essential to that determination.

I expect to provide a final embalming/no-embalming decision **within seven (7) days** of this letter.

Please:

1. **Confirm receipt of this letter in writing** (email is acceptable) within 24 hours.
2. **Document on his file** that embalming is on hold pending family instruction.
3. **Notify me immediately** if any condition develops that, in your professional judgment, requires action sooner — and do not act unilaterally; contact me first.
4. **Provide me, at my request, the current refrigeration log and chain-of-custody records** for the remains.

I appreciate your professional handling of this matter.

Sincerely,

## Notes for the family (do not include in the letter)

- **Call first, before sending the letter.** Most funeral homes will respect a phone call to hold embalming, but the written instruction creates the legal record. Both.
- **In Oregon and Washington, the funeral home cannot embalm without family authorization** (with narrow public-health exceptions). They are required to follow your written instruction.
- **The "seven days" line is intentional.** It gives you a real deadline to make the autopsy decision without leaving it open-ended (which makes funeral homes nervous about long-term cold-storage logistics).
- **If the funeral home pushes back** ("we need to embalm for transport / for visitation / per state law / whatever") — ask them to put the legal/regulatory citation in writing. There are very few situations where they actually have authority to override the family's written instruction.
- **If you are out of state** (e.g., decedent died in OR but family is in WA, body is being transported), this gets more complicated — embalming may be required for some interstate transport. **In that case, contact the medical examiner FIRST** so they can arrange transport to the autopsy facility before any embalming happens.
