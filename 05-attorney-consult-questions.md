## Questions to ask EVERY firm (use the same questions for all three so answers are comparable)

### Experience
1. **How many wrongful-death medical-malpractice cases have you tried to verdict in the last 5 years?**
2. **How many of those involved orthopedic surgery / chronic infection / hospital-acquired infection?**
3. **How many cases have you brought against Legacy Health system specifically?** What were the outcomes (settlement / verdict / dismissal)?

### Strategy
4. **Based on what I have described — sudden death after 18 surgeries with chronic infection, one week from final pin removal, no prodrome — what are the strongest theories of liability you would investigate first?**
5. **Will you commit to obtaining a formal pre-suit medical-expert review by an independent orthopedic surgeon and an independent infectious-disease specialist?**
6. **How will you handle the autopsy results — do you have a pathologist you work with regularly?**

### Money & costs
7. **What is your contingency rate?** (Typical range: 33% pre-suit, 40% post-filing, sometimes higher post-trial.)
8. **Will you front the case-cost retainer (expert-witness fees, court costs, deposition costs)?** How much do you typically advance? When does that get repaid?
9. **If we lose at trial, what do we owe you?** (In Oregon, Olin v. Lehman and similar cases have shaped this — most contingency contracts say "no recovery, no fee, but client may be responsible for costs." Make sure this is in writing.)

### Cap & damages
10. **Oregon's $500K noneconomic damages cap (ORS 31.710) still applies in wrongful death after Greist v. Phillips (1995) and Busch v. McInnis (2020). Walk me through how you would build out economic damages — lost earnings, lost services, lost contributions to the family — to make this case viable economically given the cap.**

### Conflicts and case-load
11. **Do you have any current or recent representation involving Legacy Health system, or relationships with any defense firm representing Legacy?**
12. **What is your current case load? How many active wrongful-death cases are you handling? Who specifically will be working on this case — partner, associate, paralegal?**

### Timeline
13. **What is your projected timeline — pre-suit investigation, expert review, complaint filing, discovery, mediation, trial?** (Typical OR med-mal: 18–30 months from intake to resolution.)
14. **What can the family do today, this week, this month, that materially helps the case?**

### The hard one
15. **Look at what I've described and tell me honestly: do you think this case is worth pursuing, or do you think we are likely to find that this was an unavoidable complication of a complicated patient and that no liability case exists?** *(A good plaintiff-side attorney will tell you "I don't know yet, but here is what we need to find out." A bad one will either oversell ["definitely a case!"] or undersell ["these are hard, sorry"]. Listen for the calibrated answer.)*

## Red flags in any consult

- **Promises of a specific dollar outcome** ("we'll get you $5 million"). Nobody can promise that.
- **Pressure to retain immediately** before you've consulted other firms.
- **Vague answers to questions about case load or who will work the case.**
- **Reluctance to put the contingency rate, costs, and end-of-case settlement language in writing.**
- **Refusal to commit to an expert review before filing.** A good firm reviews with experts before they sue. A bad firm files first and sorts out later.
- **Unfamiliarity with Oregon's $500K cap or the Busch v. McInnis ruling.** These are bread-and-butter for any OR med-mal attorney.

## Green flags

- **They ask hard questions back at you.** Did he have any underlying conditions? Any prior cardiac history? Was the family aware of any known infection-risk discussions?
- **They want to see the autopsy report before evaluating the case.** Good firms wait for objective evidence.
- **They explain why this kind of case is hard.** Wrongful-death med-mal in Oregon, with a $500K noneconomic cap, requires substantial economic damages to be economically viable. A firm that lays this out honestly is a firm that will tell you the truth at every stage.

## After the three consults

Pick the firm that:
1. Has the most direct experience with the specific issues (orthopedic infection, hospital-acquired sepsis, hardware-associated osteomyelitis).
2. Was most calibrated about the difficulty of the case (not over-promising, not over-discouraging).
3. Will commit in writing to the costs structure you can live with.
4. The family feels comfortable working with for 2+ years. This is a long process. The relationship matters.

**Get the engagement agreement in writing.** Read it. If anything is unclear, ask. If they push back on questions, that is the answer.
