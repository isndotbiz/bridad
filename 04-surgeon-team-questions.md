---

**1. VTE prophylaxis at home.**
What anticoagulant — drug, dose, frequency, start date, stop date — was prescribed for [PATIENT FULL NAME] for the period covering his most recent surgery through the date of death? Was the prescription filled? Was patient compliance documented? Was a SCD/compression device prescribed in addition to or in place of pharmacologic prophylaxis?

**2. Most recent echocardiogram.**
Date and type (transthoracic or transesophageal) of the most recent echocardiogram performed during his treatment course. What were the findings — particularly regarding valvular vegetations, valvular dysfunction, aortic-root pathology, or chamber dilation? If no echocardiogram was performed during the multi-year course of his chronic-osteomyelitis treatment, please confirm that explicitly.

**3. Blood-culture history.**
For each of the 18 surgical encounters and each separate clinic episode addressing infection: were blood cultures drawn? On what dates? What organisms grew? What sensitivities? Were any cultures drawn within 30 days of his death? What were the most recent CRP, ESR, and procalcitonin values?

**4. Antibiotic regimen at time of death.**
Complete list of antibiotics, antifungals, and antivirals he was prescribed in the 30 days before death — drug, dose, frequency, route, duration. Specifically: was he on any of azithromycin, clarithromycin, erythromycin, ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin? Was he co-administered ondansetron, methadone, or any other QT-prolonging agent? What were his most recent serum potassium and magnesium values? When was his most recent ECG, and what was the QTc interval?

**5. Last clinic visit.**
Date of the most recent clinic visit before death. Vital signs (BP, HR, temperature, oxygen saturation). Examination findings. Wound and pin-site status. Any concerning lab trends (CRP, ESR, WBC, creatinine, hemoglobin) — and if any were trending in the wrong direction, what plan was put in place?

**6. Surgeon and team training.**
What is your fellowship training in deformity correction, limb lengthening, or external fixation (Ilizarov / Taylor Spatial Frame methodology)? How many TSF or Ilizarov cases per year does your team perform? Was a fellowship-trained limb-reconstruction surgeon involved in this case at any stage? If not, was referral to a tertiary limb-reconstruction center (OHSU, Seattle Children's, Harborview) ever discussed with the patient or family?

**7. Infection management plan.**
Was an infectious-disease consultant involved in the management of his chronic infection? When? What antibiotic-suppression plan was in place between surgical encounters? What was the criterion for definitive infection eradication versus chronic suppression?

**8. Pre-operative cardiac risk stratification.**
Before his most recent surgical procedure, was a pre-operative cardiac risk stratification performed? Revised Cardiac Risk Index score? Stress test? Echocardiogram? What was the risk-stratification result and how did it inform the decision to proceed?

**9. Discharge planning and home care.**
Was home health, outpatient PT, or home wound-care prescribed at the most recent discharge? Was the patient given written DVT-prophylaxis education? Pin-care instructions? Was a follow-up phone call from your office documented in the week between his most recent encounter and his death?

**10. Indication for the original procedure.**
What was the original indication for the limb-reconstruction work — trauma, congenital deformity, post-infection bone loss, malunion, other? When was the first surgery performed at Legacy Emanuel? Was the option of amputation with prosthetic reconstruction discussed at any point during the 18-surgery course? Was the patient counseled about the cumulative-risk profile of an extended fixator course?

## Notes for the family (do not include)

- **The surgeon will likely route this to risk management.** That is normal. A response from risk management or in-house counsel is still a response, and it goes in the file.
- **If the surgeon declines to answer**, that is itself useful — it is the kind of fact a med-mal attorney specifically wants to know.
- **Do not be surprised if responses are short, hedged, or refer to the medical record.** Save them all. Compare them against the records when those arrive.
- **Question 6 (training) and Question 2 (echocardiogram) are the highest-leverage.** A surgeon without limb-reconstruction fellowship training doing 18 surgeries on a TSF case, and never ordering an echocardiogram on a patient with multi-year hardware-associated bacteremia, are independently defensible standard-of-care criticisms. The attorney will start there.
