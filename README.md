# After a Sudden Death During Long-Course Limb Reconstruction — Action Packet

A research-backed, ready-to-send document set for a family navigating the aftermath of a sudden death during or shortly after a complex external-fixator limb-reconstruction case (Taylor Spatial Frame / Ilizarov). Built around a real case at Legacy Emanuel Medical Center in Portland, Oregon — a 60-year-old man in good health overall, 18 surgeries fighting chronic infection, one week from final pin removal, who collapsed on his porch with no warning. Generalized so any family facing a similar situation in Oregon or Washington can adapt it.

**Date of research:** 2026-05-08. All source URLs verified live at that date.

---

## What this packet does

Seven documents the family can fill in and send. They lock down the evidence base before it disappears:

- A no-embalming letter to the funeral home
- A demand-autopsy letter to the county Medical Examiner, with phone scripts and private-autopsy fallbacks
- A HIPAA right-of-access records request with **litigation-hold language**
- Ten direct questions for the operating surgeon, certified mail
- A scripted protocol for free consultations with three Portland medical-malpractice attorneys
- A master action checklist with statute deadlines and red flags
- This README, summarizing the underlying findings

Total: ~600 lines, source-verified, no hedge padding.

---

## The single most time-critical action

**Tell the funeral home — TODAY, in writing — REFRIGERATE ONLY, NO EMBALMING.** Embalming permanently destroys toxicology evidence (which is needed if antibiotic-induced arrhythmia killed him) and makes sepsis/bacteremia diagnosis "nearly impossible." Best autopsy window is 24–48 hours; a refrigerated body is usable for several days, sometimes a week. Document #1 is the letter for that. Send it before anything else.

## 2. The medical reality — external fixator, distraction osteogenesis, chronic infection

**What the X-ray shows.** A ring fixator on a long bone — almost certainly a **Taylor Spatial Frame (TSF)** or classic **Ilizarov** apparatus. The technique, **distraction osteogenesis**, slowly grows new bone over months while the frame holds the bone in place. Wires and pins traverse skin into bone. Pin-site issues are constant; deep complications are uncommon but consequential.

**Pin-site and deep-infection rates (peer-reviewed literature):**

- **Pin-tract infection (PTI):** 0.5%–30% in tighter studies; up to 96% in some series with broad definitions. The wide range reflects definitional heterogeneity.
- **Severe PTI:** approximately 4%.
- **Deep infection:** approximately 4%.
- **Chronic osteomyelitis:** approximately 4% in limb-lengthening cohorts. Roughly 7.3% of pin-tract infections progress to osteomyelitis in some series.
- **Most common organism:** *Staphylococcus aureus* (≈47%), with rising MRSA prevalence; then *S. epidermidis*, *E. coli*, and *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*. Biofilm formation on hardware is the driver of treatment failure — biofilms are why antibiotics alone don't clear hardware-associated osteo, and why hardware removal is often the only definitive cure.

**Is 18 surgeries high?**

- Median revision count in distraction-osteogenesis cohorts is **2 (range 0–11)** in published series, prior to advanced biologic salvage.
- A 10-year tertiary-center BOA/BAPRAS audit found revision rates of approximately 56% for soft-tissue and bone complications.
- **18 surgeries sits at the high end of what published case series describe.** It usually signals one of three patterns: (a) catastrophic initial trauma, (b) refractory / recurrent infection driving repeat washouts, or (c) failure of earlier reconstruction requiring escalating procedures. All three warrant chart-level investigation.

**DVT/VTE prophylaxis for outpatients with leg external fixators**

- No external-fixator-specific guideline exists. ACCP and AAOS guidelines cover hip and knee arthroplasty and hip-fracture cases, with extended outpatient prophylaxis (LMWH or DOAC) recommended for **10–35 days post-op**.
- For prolonged immobilization with a leg fixator and ongoing infection (which this case clearly was), **standard of care defaults to extended pharmacologic prophylaxis as long as mobility is impaired** — typically enoxaparin (Lovenox) or a DOAC (rivaroxaban, apixaban) — unless contraindicated by bleeding risk.
- **The single highest-yield question for the surgical team:** what was the prescribed VTE prophylaxis at home, and was it filled and being taken?

## 3. Sudden-death mechanism analysis — ranked, not a verdict

**Case features that drive the ranking:**

- 60-year-old male, otherwise healthy.
- Chronic indwelling hardware (ring fixator) on a leg.
- Ongoing infection through the entire 18-surgery course.
- One week from final pin removal.
- **Collapsed at home, no prodrome — no fever, no chest pain, no shortness of breath, no warning.**
- Likely mild exertion (porch implies standing or walking).

This pattern has a textbook differential. Without an autopsy, no mechanism is confirmed. Anyone who states a definitive cause is guessing.

| Hypothesis | Fit | Resolves with |
|---|---|---|
| **Pulmonary embolism** | HIGHEST — every classic risk factor present | Autopsy lung dissection + VTE-prophylaxis chart record |
| **Infective endocarditis with septic coronary embolism** | HIGH if no recent echocardiogram on file | Autopsy heart valves + last echo date + culture history |
| **QT-drug-induced sudden cardiac arrhythmia** | MED if azithromycin / fluoroquinolones / ondansetron + low K/Mg on board | Med list at death + last ECG QTc + electrolytes |
| **Acute MI from chronic-inflammation–driven plaque destabilization** | MED contributor | Autopsy coronaries + IL-6 / CRP trend |
| **Septic decompensation** | LOW given no prodrome | Last 24h vitals — effectively excluded if all normal |

**(A) Pulmonary embolism — best fit.** Post-orthopedic-surgery PE incidence is **0.7%–30%; ~2% cumulative for major orthopedic surgery; up to 24% in hip-fracture-repair cohorts.** Mechanism: chronic immobility + recent surgery + hypercoagulability from chronic inflammation + age 60 → DVT → embolization → acute right-ventricular failure → sudden cardiovascular collapse. Classic presentation is **no prodrome.** Many fatal PEs present as syncope or sudden death triggered by mild exertion (Valsalva, standing up, walking onto a porch). Mortality of perioperative PE is ~12.9% in the hip-fracture cohort.

**(B) Infective endocarditis with septic coronary embolism.** Eighteen surgeries fighting infection on indwelling hardware = chronic intermittent bacteremia = highest-risk substrate for IE seeding. **IE mortality runs 20%–25% overall.** Embolization complicates 22%–50% of IE cases. **Septic coronary embolism is rare (~1.5% of IE cases) but two-thirds fatal when it happens** — sudden cardiac death from coronary occlusion by a vegetation fragment, no chest-pain prodrome required if the occlusion is total and abrupt. Other sudden-death mechanisms in IE: vegetation rupture causing acute valvular incompetence, conduction-block from aortic-root abscess, septic stroke. **The chart-question that resolves this:** when was the last echocardiogram, and were blood cultures drawn during any of the 18 admissions? Absence of an echo in a multi-year hardware-bacteremia patient is a defensible standard-of-care criticism.

**(C) Antibiotic-induced sudden cardiac arrhythmia.** FDA black-box and label warnings exist for azithromycin, clarithromycin, erythromycin, ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, and moxifloxacin. Azithromycin: ~47 excess cardiovascular deaths per million 5-day courses vs. amoxicillin in the NEJM 2012 Tennessee Medicaid study. Among fluoroquinolones, **moxifloxacin carries the highest QT/torsades risk; ciprofloxacin the lowest.** Risk multiplies with hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, female sex, bradycardia, age, structural heart disease, and concomitant QT-prolonging drugs (ondansetron is the easy-to-miss one — frequently given for post-op nausea, also QT-prolonging). **Embalming will permanently destroy the toxicology evidence for this hypothesis.** That alone is a sufficient reason to insist on autopsy before embalming.

**(D) Acute MI from chronic inflammation.** **Severe infection elevates MI risk for up to 10 years post-infection.** Chronic IL-6 elevation from a multi-year osteomyelitis fight is independently associated with sudden cardiac death. Mechanism: inflammation-driven plaque destabilization, hypercoagulability, endothelial dysfunction. Fit is weaker than PE or IE because type-I MI usually has some cardiac prodrome; sudden-collapse-without-warning suggests arrhythmic death (which can still be triggered by acute MI but more often points toward A or B).

**(E) Septic decompensation.** Septic shock virtually always has a prodrome: fever, tachycardia, malaise, mental-status change. The case explicitly excludes these. **Verify** by asking whether vitals were taken in the 24 hours before death, and what they were. Otherwise this hypothesis is effectively excluded.

**Bottom line:** the no-prodrome sudden death of an otherwise-healthy 60-year-old with chronic hardware infection, recent surgery, and an immobilized leg is most consistent with **pulmonary embolism**, with **infective endocarditis with septic coronary embolism** as the next-strongest hypothesis, and **antibiotic-induced QT-prolongation arrhythmia** as a real third possibility that **embalming destroys the evidence for.** An autopsy is the single highest-leverage action — without one, all five hypotheses remain alive and the family loses both medical and legal clarity.

## 4. Legal landscape — Oregon and Washington

### Oregon (where treatment occurred — controlling jurisdiction)

- **ORS 12.110(4) — medical malpractice:** **2-year discovery rule** from when the injury was discovered or reasonably should have been discovered. **5-year statute of repose** from the date of treatment or omission. Beyond 5 years, no claim regardless of when discovered, except for fraud or concealment which extends 2 years from discovery of the fraud.
- **ORS 12.115 — absolute repose:** 10 years from the act or omission for any negligent-injury claim.
- **ORS 30.020 — wrongful death:** **3 years from the date of death.** This is the operative deadline for the wrongful-death claim itself.
- **ORS 31.710 — noneconomic damages cap: $500,000.** *This is the most-misreported point.* **Greist v. Phillips (1995)** — Oregon Supreme Court upheld the cap **in wrongful death cases.** **Busch v. McInnis Waste Systems (2020)** invalidated the cap **only for personal-injury (non-death) cases.** **The $500K noneconomic cap STILL APPLIES in Oregon wrongful-death medical-malpractice cases.** Economic damages — lost wages, lost services, lost contributions to family, medical bills, funeral costs — remain **uncapped.** The economic-damages proof is what makes a wrongful-death case viable.
- **ORS chapter 146 — medical examiner:** the Oregon ME has investigative jurisdiction over deaths "unexpectedly while in apparent good health" or not under the immediate care of a physician. This case plainly qualifies. The ME may decline to autopsy citing apparent natural cause; the family does not have to accept that passively.
- **ORS 192.563 — patient records:** providers must respond to written records requests within 30 days. HIPAA federal rule overrides any state fee that exceeds the federal "reasonable cost-based" cap.

### Washington (residence jurisdiction — likely not controlling here, but document)

- **RCW 4.16.350 — medical malpractice:** **3 years from act or omission OR 1 year from discovery, whichever is longer. 8-year statute of repose.**
- **RCW 4.20.046 — survival actions:** preserves the decedent's pre-death claims for the estate.
- **RCW 70.02.080 — patient records:** providers must respond within **15 working days** (extendable to 21 working days for unusual circumstances). Statutory copy fee: $0.65/page first 30 pages, $0.50/page thereafter, plus up to $15 clerical fee.

### Choice of forum

Oregon courts have ruled that Oregon does not have jurisdiction over Washington providers for malpractice in Washington against Washington residents — and the mirror logic protects Oregon providers similarly. **In this case the treatment occurred in Oregon (Legacy Emanuel) — Oregon is the proper venue.** Washington patient residency does not change that; Oregon law and the $500K cap will apply. Counsel must be Oregon-licensed plaintiff medical-malpractice counsel; Washington counsel can co-counsel but cannot lead.
